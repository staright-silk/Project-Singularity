Project Singularity: A Numerical Study of Hawking Like Radiation in a Controlled Wave Packet Model

Abstract

This paper presents Project Singularity as a numerical framework for studying a simplified black hole environment in which a quantum wave packet propagates through a Schwarzschild inspired potential and is coupled to a stochastic source near the horizon. The purpose of the work is not to reproduce the full formalism of quantum gravity, but to construct an interpretable and computationally tractable model that captures several of the central conceptual features associated with black hole evaporation. These include horizon dependent scattering, partial absorption near the event horizon, and a radiation like signal that is treated as a phenomenological analogue of Hawking emission. The model is implemented in both a live web based simulation and a standalone Python engine, allowing the user to inspect probability evolution, flux-like diagnostics, and absorption behavior over time. The significance of the project lies in its ability to connect abstract theoretical intuition with concrete numerical experimentation, showing how a reduced model can reveal the qualitative structure of semiclassical black hole physics without requiring a complete theory of quantum spacetime.

Introduction

The study of black hole thermodynamics has become one of the most influential threads in modern theoretical physics because it fuses several major domains of inquiry, including gravitation, quantum field theory, thermodynamics, and information theory. Hawking radiation, in particular, transformed the way black holes are understood by showing that quantum processes near the event horizon can cause a black hole to emit radiation and gradually lose mass. Although the result is now widely regarded as a landmark achievement, the derivation depends on a semiclassical treatment in which quantum fields propagate on a classical curved background. This makes the result both conceptually profound and mathematically delicate. For that reason, numerical models that approximate the underlying behavior can serve an important role. They make it possible to investigate similar phenomena in a controlled setting, test the sensitivity of the dynamics to various parameters, and communicate the broader logic of the theory to audiences who may not be trained in the full formal apparatus.

Project Singularity was developed with that goal in mind. The project models a one dimensional radial wavefunction outside a Schwarzschild like horizon using a discrete numerical grid and a complex valued wave packet. The initial state is a normalized Gaussian wave packet, which is then propagated through an effective potential that resembles the radial structure expected near a black hole. A stochastic near horizon source is introduced to imitate the idea of small fluctuating emission processes. The model is deliberately simplified, but it is not arbitrary. Its design is guided by the same broad conceptual ingredients that appear in semiclassical treatments of black hole evaporation: a horizon scale, a scattering environment, a nontrivial potential, and a mechanism for outgoing radiation-like behavior. As such, the project functions not as a replacement for full physical theory, but as an accessible and explainable computational laboratory for exploring the qualitative behavior of horizon-adjacent quantum dynamics.

Theoretical Motivation

The motivation for Project Singularity derives from the broader effort to understand how quantum mechanics behaves in curved spacetime. In the standard semiclassical framework, the background geometry is treated classically while matter fields are quantized. Near an event horizon, this treatment leads to particle production that is thermal in character and associated with the surface gravity of the black hole. The resulting emission is often described as a quantum effect arising from the vacuum structure of the field, even though the black hole itself does not emit particles in the ordinary sense. Instead, the outgoing radiation appears as a consequence of the nontrivial relation between the quantum state defined at infinity and the state defined near the horizon.

This conceptual picture is difficult to represent directly in a simple numerical setting. A full treatment would require solving quantum field theory on curved spacetime, a task that is far beyond the scope of a compact simulation platform. However, the essential ingredients of the picture can be approximated. The wave packet represents the propagation of a quantum excitation. The effective potential represents the influence of the gravitational background on that propagation. The absorbing boundary layer represents the loss of probability that can occur as waves move away from the region of interest. The stochastic source represents a phenomenological emission mechanism that mimics the irregularity and limited strength of radiation-like behavior near the horizon. These ideas are not identical to the exact physics of Hawking radiation, but they preserve the same conceptual architecture and make the system suitable for educational, exploratory, and computational study.

Methodological Framework

The central dynamical quantity in the simulation is the complex wavefunction, denoted by psi(r, t). The probability density is obtained from the squared magnitude of this wavefunction, and the evolution is governed by an equation of Schrödinger type with a spatially varying potential. The numerical method used to propagate the field is a split operator approach, in which the kinetic and potential contributions are treated in separate stages. First, the wavefunction is multiplied by a phase factor derived from the effective potential. Second, the wavefunction is transformed into Fourier space and advanced under the kinetic operator. Third, the potential phase factor is applied again. This procedure is widely used in quantum simulations because it offers numerical stability and computational efficiency while preserving the essential structure of wave propagation.

The spatial grid is defined over a radial domain located just outside the event horizon. The initial packet is placed away from the horizon and given an initial momentum so that it approaches the region of interest. The wave packet is normalized so that its integrated probability remains finite and its dynamics can be interpreted in a meaningful way. The effective potential is based on a Schwarzschild inspired expression that depends on the horizon scale, represented by the Schwarzschild radius. This potential introduces a barrier and curvature-like structure that changes how the packet scatters and interacts with the near horizon region. Although it is not a full solution of the Einstein field equations, it captures the qualitative idea that the region near the horizon behaves differently from the outer domain.

To prevent artificial reflections from the numerical boundaries, the model includes an absorbing layer. This layer damps the wavefunction near the edges of the domain so that outgoing probability does not bounce back and contaminate the interior dynamics. The absorbing mechanism is not meant to represent a physical horizon or an actual event boundary, but rather to enforce an effective outflow condition. This is especially important in a finite domain because without such a mechanism, the simulation would gradually accumulate interference patterns that would complicate the interpretation of the results. The absorbing layer thus serves as both a numerical necessity and a conceptual analogue of irreversible loss.

The project also includes a Hawking like source near the horizon. Rather than deriving particle production from first principles, the model inserts a small stochastic perturbation into the wavefunction within a narrow spatial region just outside the Schwarzschild radius. The disturbance is complex valued, meaning it carries both amplitude and phase variation, and its magnitude is controlled by a temperature like parameter and a width parameter. The source is refreshed at each time step with a randomized phase, creating an irregular yet controlled emission-like feature. This construction is intentionally phenomenological. Its purpose is to emulate the presence of fluctuating near horizon activity while preserving numerical tractability and interpretability. It is equivalent to a reduced model of the radiation process, not a direct derivation of Hawking emission from quantum field theory.

Numerical Implementation and Architecture

Project Singularity is implemented in two complementary forms. The first is a standalone Python engine that performs the numerical evolution directly. The second is a web based application in which a FastAPI backend solves the simulation and a React frontend visualizes the results in real time. This dual implementation is significant because it allows the project to serve both research and demonstration purposes. The backend owns the simulation state, advances the wavefunction over time, and exposes its parameters and diagnostics through a WebSocket and REST interface. The frontend then displays the evolving density profile, potential profile, and emission-like signal as interactive graphs and traces.

The backend workflow is straightforward. The simulation is initialized with a set of physical and numerical parameters, including the Schwarzschild radius, grid length, grid resolution, timestep, packet width, packet momentum, temperature-like source amplitude, and radiation width. The solver then advances the wavefunction through repeated time steps, updating the state and collecting diagnostic quantities such as retained probability and absorbed probability. The frontend receives periodic state updates and visualizes the current simulation frame. In this way, the system combines the rigor of a numerical solver with the accessibility of a live interactive interface.

The simulation broadcast rate and time stepping are also carefully structured. The backend advances the system over a fixed number of numerical steps per broadcast interval, allowing the visual interface to remain responsive without sacrificing the underlying physical update rate. This architecture makes the project suitable for live experimentation and also ensures that the underlying numerical process remains consistent with the intended model. The design reflects a broader principle in scientific computing: numerical models become more useful when the underlying solver and the user-facing representation are clearly separated.

Results and Interpretation

The simulation yields several observables that are meaningful for interpretation. The probability density trace shows how the wave packet moves across the domain and how its shape changes as it interacts with the potential and the near horizon source. The emitted signal is represented by a flux-like diagnostic derived from the norm of the stochastic source, while the captured probability measure estimates the amount of probability lost to the absorbing region. These diagnostics are not presented as exact measurements of Hawking radiation. Instead, they are intended to convey the qualitative behavior of a horizon-adjacent scattering process that includes both propagation and loss. In this sense, the simulation is less a precision model than a structured instrument for exploring the logic of a radiation-like process.

The results are sensitive to the model parameters in ways that are physically intuitive. Increasing the horizon scale changes the effective potential and modifies the scattering environment. A larger black hole-like radius produces a different radial barrier and changes the degree to which the packet interacts with the region near the horizon. Increasing the temperature-like source amplitude strengthens the stochastic disturbance and produces a more pronounced emission-like signal. Broadening the emission region modifies the spatial distribution of the source, changing the visible footprint of the near horizon activity. Changing the packet momentum alters the timing and character of the interaction, because the wave packet approaches the horizon region with different kinetic energy. These relationships demonstrate that the model is not merely computationally convenient; it is also parameterically interpretable.

This interpretability is one of the principal strengths of the project. In many numerical simulations, especially those based on highly coupled or opaque systems, it can be difficult to determine whether a change in output reflects a meaningful physical effect or an artifact of the implementation. In Project Singularity, the influence of each key parameter can be traced back to a specific mechanism in the model. The horizon radius affects the potential. The source amplitude affects the excitation strength. The absorber affects the loss rate. The grid and timestep determine the numerical resolution. Because these factors are explicitly identifiable, the simulation functions as a transparent model rather than a black box.

The web based implementation further reinforces this transparency. The browser client allows a user to observe the wavefunction and the diagnostics in real time, making the system easier to understand than a static set of simulation outputs. The visual separation between the probability density, the potential profile, and the emission-like signal helps the user see that the system contains multiple interacting components rather than a single undifferentiated process. This visual architecture is pedagogically valuable because it exposes the model’s logic in a way that is difficult to achieve from equations alone.

Discussion

Project Singularity should be understood as a reduced model rather than a definitive physical theory. Its value lies in its ability to preserve the conceptual structure of a semiclassical black hole environment while remaining simple enough to compute, explain, and visualize. The model does not claim to resolve the full quantum gravitational problem, nor does it attempt to reproduce the precise thermodynamic spectrum of Hawking radiation. Instead, it captures a set of qualitative features that are central to the broader discussion: a horizon scale, a wave-like quantum excitation, an effective potential, and a radiation-like emission mechanism. In that respect, it serves as a bridge between conceptual physics and numerical experimentation.

There are, however, important limitations that should be acknowledged. The potential is a phenomenological approximation rather than a derivation from general relativity. The stochastic source is not derived from quantum field theory, and the emitted signal should not be interpreted as a measured astrophysical flux. The absorbing boundary is a numerical device rather than a physical mechanism. These limitations are not weaknesses in the project so much as deliberate design choices. The project is meant to be useful precisely because it strips away the complexity of full quantum gravity and exposes the essential structure of the phenomenon in a simplified setting.

The significance of the work is therefore methodological as much as scientific. By combining a simple wave-based evolution scheme with a horizon-inspired potential and a radiation-like source, the project demonstrates how abstract ideas in theoretical physics can be translated into an accessible computational form. It also shows that even highly simplified models can provide insights into the way physical systems behave under conditions of reflection, absorption, and emission. This makes the project relevant not only to black hole research but also to broader topics in scientific computing, visualization, and education.

Conclusion

Project Singularity offers a compact and explainable numerical platform for exploring black hole like dynamics through a simplified quantum wave packet model. Its value lies not in claiming a complete description of quantum gravity, but in demonstrating how a small set of well chosen ingredients can produce behavior that resembles the conceptual structure of Hawking radiation. By combining a Schwarzschild inspired potential, split operator time evolution, absorbing boundaries, and a stochastic near horizon source, the project provides a useful bridge between theoretical intuition and computational experimentation. It is a model of limited scope, but one that is rigorous enough to support exploration, visualization, and educational engagement with the broader physics of black holes. In that respect, Project Singularity serves not only as a simulation tool but also as a clear example of how scientific ideas can be translated into an understandable and interactive computational form.
